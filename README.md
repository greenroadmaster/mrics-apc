## Where It Started: A Street in Italy

"Pasta 25 euro? Bullshit!"

That was my reaction sitting at a small *trattoria* on a Ligurian street corner, years ago.

The cost to make it was probably €11-12. The price was €25. By pure cost logic, I was being robbed.

But when it arrived—a simple bowl of **Aglio e Olio** with warm bread under Mediterranean sun—something shifted. I wasn't just eating pasta. I was tasting 30 years of the same chef doing the same thing the same way. The morning market. The sourced garlic. The stone buildings. The ease of sitting there knowing this exact bowl had been served this exact way for decades.

I learned that day: **price and cost are not the same thing.**

---

## How My Understanding Evolved

### The Early Years: Cost Discipline

When I started in construction, I was a cost person. What did things actually cost? Material per unit. Labor per hour. Overhead per percentage. I verified everything. I cut waste. I challenged every number.

That was necessary. It still is.

But after 20+ years across APAC—managing USD 900M projects, certifying monthly claims, negotiating thousands of variations—I realized something: **cost discipline alone isn't enough.**

### What Changed

I began working on both sides of the table.

Sometimes I was the buyer—verifying contractor quotes, challenging waste, protecting budgets. Pure cost focus. Necessary.

But increasingly, I was the seller—quoting my own services. And I couldn't price them at cost. Because what was I really providing? Not just my time. My RICS credential. 20+ years of APAC judgment. Tools mastery. Professional insurance covering my mistakes. Accountability.

If I'd priced at cost, I would have undervalued everything I'd learned.

And that's when I understood the Italian pasta shop.

### What I Know Now

**Cost** is what it actually costs.  
**Value** is what it's worth to the person buying it.

A concrete slab costs RM 800/m² to build correctly.  
A budget apartment sells for RM 900/m².  
A premium data centre sells for RM 1,500/m².  

Same building. Same cost. Different value—because one buyer values affordability, another values reliability, another values prestige.

My job isn't to pick a side. It's to understand both.

When I evaluate a contractor's quote, I'm ruthless on cost—because waste hurts the project.  
When I price my services, I charge for value—because undercutting hurts the profession.

Both are right. Both serve integrity.

---

## What This Means

I don't apologize for either position.

The contractor who says "my cost is RM 50K, market rate is RM 52K, I'll quote RM 52K" isn't being greedy—they're being professional.

The QS who quotes RM 65K for services that cost RM 40K isn't being exploitative—they're pricing their judgment.

And the client who pays RM 65K isn't being foolish—they're buying competence, not just labor.

This is what 20 years taught me: **Cost discipline and value creation aren't opposites. They're complementary.**
